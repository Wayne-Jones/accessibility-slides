Why Accessibility is Important

Inaccessible websites means that people with disabilities are denied equal access to get information.

It can exclude people just as much as a physical location that has steps at an entrance. In a way excluding a person digitally is in a way a form of discrimination

Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)

Legislation passed in 1990 and amended in 2008, is a federal civil rights law that prohibits discrimination against people with disabilities in everyday activities.

The ADA applies to State and Local Goverments (Title II) as well as Businesses that are open to the public (Title III).

Under Title III specifically, no individual may be discriminated against on the basis of disability with regards to the full and equal enjoyment of the goods, services, facilities, or accommodations of any place of public accommodation by any person who owns, leases, or operates a place of public accommodation.

While not specificially mentioning websites in the Legislation, through court precedence, this act is assumed to extend beyond the physical space of businesses and governments - which would mean digital spaces including websites. Though in some circuit courts the website would have to be connected to a physical location whereas other circuit courts you can bring a lawsuit against an online-only business

Over the years there has been an increasing number of Title III lawsuits and in 2020 alone there has been more than 2500 lawsuits regarding inaccessible websites

Source: Federal Website Accessibility Lawsuits Increased in 2020 Despite Mid-Year Pandemic Lull

Disability Facts

According to the 2021 American Community Survey, out of an estimated 320M people

  • 42M people live with disabilities
    • 11M (3.6%) people live with visual impairments
    • 8M (2.5%) people live with hearing impairments

Source: 2021 ACS Disability Characteristics

In WebAIM's Screen Reader User Survey in 2021, of those surveyed,

  • 92.3% of respondents use a screen reader due to a disability
  • 46.3% of those said they used their primary screen reader due to existing comfort/expertise followed by 29.5% said they use it for the features
  • Increasing trend of screen reader usage on mobile devices from 67% in 2010 to 90% in 2021
  • On Desktop popular screen readers include: JAWS, NVDA, followed by VoiceOver. On Mobile, Voiceover was the most popular and heavily used followed by Talkback and Voice Assistant

Source: WebAIM Screen Reader Survey #9

Common Barriers for Accessibility

Time To Market - Because its a race to develop a fast viable working product. Accessibility is the last thing on companies minds

Knowing how to test - One common complaint is that developers don't know how to test for accessibility using assistive readers or tools

Time it takes to test - Another complaint is that companies say that it takes a while to build for accessibility. But if you build a website with accessibility in mind, it won't take up any more time to develop.

Why improve on things you can't see - Just because you can't visually see the changes, doesn't mean that it wouldn't impact the web experience for others

Semantic HTML

Semantic elements clearly describes it's meaning to both the browser and developer

Non Semantic Elements: div and span

Semantic Elements: header, footer, nav, and main

Some semantic elements can be used to describe the different parts of a web page

For screen reader users, using semantic elements the browser can clearly articulate to the user what HTML element they are on, if it's a heading they can navigate by headings. If it's a list item, it can describe how many items are in the list.
