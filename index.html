Why Accessibility is Important

Inaccessible websites means that people with disabilities are denied equal access to get information.

Companies can be sued for not maintaining an accessible website

Inaccessible websites exclude people just as much as a physical location that has steps at an entrance. In a way excluding a person digitally is in a way a form of discrimination.

In addition, in the United States, companies could be held liable for failing to create an accessible website due to the Americans with Disabilities Act.

Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)

ADA (enact. 1990, amend. 2008) is a federal civil rights law that prohibits discrimination against people with disabilities in everyday activities.

The ADA applies to State and Local Goverments (Title II) as well as Businesses that are open to the public (Title III).

In 2020 alone there has been more than 2500 Title III lawsuits regarding inaccessible websites

Source: Federal Website Accessibility Lawsuits Increased in 2020 Despite Mid-Year Pandemic Lull

Under Title III specifically, no individual may be discriminated against on the basis of disability with regards to the full and equal enjoyment of the goods, services, facilities, or accommodations of any place of public accommodation by any person who owns, leases, or operates a place of public accommodation.

While not specificially mentioning websites in the Legislation, through court precedence, this act is assumed to extend beyond the physical space of businesses and governments - which would mean digital spaces including websites. Though in some circuit courts the website would have to be connected to a physical location whereas other circuit courts you can bring a lawsuit against an online-only business.

There have been an increasing number of Title III lawsuits over the years.

  • Increasing trend of screen reader usage on mobile devices from 67% in 2010 to 90% in 2021
  • On Desktop popular screen readers include: JAWS, NVDA, followed by VoiceOver. On Mobile, Voiceover was the most popular and heavily used followed by Talkback and Voice Assistant

Semantic HTML

Semantic elements clearly describes it's meaning to both the browser and developer

Non Semantic Elements: div and span

Semantic Elements: header, footer, nav, and main

Some semantic elements can be used to describe the different parts of a web page
For screen reader users, using semantic elements the browser can clearly articulate to the user what HTML element they are on, if it's a heading they can navigate by headings. If it's a list item, it can describe how many items are in the list.

Alternative Text for Images

Adding Alt Text to images improves accessibility for screen reader users

In addition, having alt text on images can help improve on search ranking.

You don't need to add alt text for all images, only on images that provide context to the content or images that have text within it.
In some cases, there are companies that utilize alt text to stuff keywords for SEO purposes.

Color Contrast

For visual impaired users, having the correct color contrast on websites is vital in order for them to see your content visibily.

You can utilize WebAIM Color Contrast Picker to compare foreground and background color palettes.

You want to aim for WCAG AA compliance for both normal and large text, ideally you would want to get AAA compliance.

Multimedia Accessibility

For multimedia such as videos, it's generally good practice to have captions/subtitles for videos.

If you're using iframes or embeds from videos such as Youtube or Vimeo, you can utilize the built in captioning system to make sure your video is accessible.

Other ways to make multimedia videos accessible

Sign Language - Adding sign language so that deaf folks can get content in their native langage

Description of Visual Information - Provide description so that people who are blind and others who cannot see the video adequately get the visual information needed to understand the content.

Transcript - Providing a text version of speech and non-speech audio information. Descriptive transcripts would ideally provide text description of the visual information.

Accessible Players - Using accessible video/audio players such as AblePlayer would do most of the heavy lifting so developers don't have to use customized solutions to make a player accessible.
